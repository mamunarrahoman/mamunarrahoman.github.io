RTL-to-GDS FLOW Elaboration

RTL Generation: Since my primary focus was on the PnR flow, I intentionally chose a simple RTL design to minimize computational complexity and to facilitate a clearer understanding of the overall flow. This synchronous 4-bit ALU (alu_4bit) processes two 4-bit inputs (A and B) through three distinct, shaded sub-modules to produce an overflow-safe 8-bit output (Y). Inside the design, the arithmetic_unit concurrently computes addition (A + B) and subtraction (A - B) into registers, while the logical_unit continuously evaluates bitwise AND (A&B) and XOR (A^B) wire outputs. A central 4-to-1 multiplexer inside the control_unit monitors a 2-bit Opcode input, routing the corresponding arithmetic or logical result to an 8-bit output register on every rising edge of the system clock (posedge clk) according to a fixed functional table (00 for add, 01 for subtract, 10 for AND, and 11 for XOR).

Tool used: ModelSim ; HDL: Verilog

Placement and Routing (PnR) Flow: Conventionally, the Placement and Routing (PnR) flow starts with design import. In this implementation, however, the flow is initiated from the synthesis stage, which is integrated into the Makefile to achieve a fully automated RTL-to-GDS flow. While industrial PnR flows often incorporate several intermediate optimization stages, including pre-placement, pre-CTS, and pre-routing steps, such stages are not strictly mandatory but effective for tressing and modification in the script. Therefore, these intermediate steps are omitted in this work to maintain a simple and streamlined flow while retaining its effectiveness. This simplified approach facilitates a better understanding of the overall physical design process and reduces unnecessary complexity. The following sections present the sequence of steps involved in the complete PnR flow, as automated through the Makefile.

RTL Synthesis: To transform a coded design into a physical device, we need to perform synthesis. Through this process, the Register Transfer Level (RTL) description is converted into a technology specific (As example: 2nm, 3nm, 4nm, 5nm etc. PDK specific synthesis) gate-level representation. Major goals of synthesis are: (i) To get a gate level netlist, (ii) Inserting clock gates, (iii) logic optimization, (iv) Inserting DEF (Design Exchange Format) logic, (v) Logic equivalence between RTL and Netlist should be maintained.

Design Import: Following synthesis, the backend implementation flow, consisting of Placement and Routing (PnR), is initiated. The design is first imported into the tool by defining a set of essential variables that are utilized throughout the flow. These variables include the top module name, power and ground nets, Verilog netlist path, MMMC configuration path, and technology library file paths (LIB, LEF, TLU+, etc.). After specifying these parameters, the tool environment is initialized and configured using the appropriate tool-specific commands.

💻 Multi Mode Multi Corner Configuration

Loading...

MMMC: In the modern VLSI physical design flow, Multi-Mode Multi-Corner (MMMC) analysis is a critical methodology used to ensure a chip's reliability across diverse operational and environmental conditions. It bridges two distinct variables: Modes, which define functional states (such as sleep, test, or normal operation) via specific clock frequencies and timing constraints (SDC); and Corners, which account for physical manufacturing process variations, voltage fluctuations, and temperature swings (PVT). By integrating these scenarios into Electronic Design Automation (EDA) tools using specialized timing libraries (.lib) and parasitic RC models, MMMC allows design engineers to simultaneously analyze and optimize critical setup and hold timing constraints, preventing post-silicon failures before the chip ever goes to fabrication. For advanced chip designs, On-Chip Variation (OCV) effects are considered to improve timing robustness. This is accomplished by applying pessimistic timing constraints, ensuring that the design can tolerate process, voltage, and temperature variations while still meeting its performance requirements.

Design Initialization and Floor-planning: The physical design implementation begins with the design initialization phase, where the environment is configured by sourcing global variables from a central configuration script (config.tcl). The core design database is then instantiated via the init_design command, which imports the structural Verilog netlist, physical technology libraries (.lef), top-level cell definitions, and the Multi-Mode Multi-Corner (MMMC) timing environment files while explicitly mapping the global power and ground nets (POWER_NET and GROUND_NET). Following initialization, the initial floorplan is established utilizing the floorPlan command to define a core area of 40 um by 15.4 um with a uniform 2 um peripheral margin on all sides. Boundary constraints and physical reliability structures are then added, including the insertion of boundary/endcap cells [ DECAP4 (for left and right edge) and DECAP2 (for top and bottom edge) ] along the chip edges to prevent well-isolation rule violations, and well-tap cells placed at a regular interval of 15 um to mitigate latch-up phenomena. To prevent unwanted special routing extensions over these peripheral decap structures, localized routing blockages are created on the Metal1 layer. Finally, logical-to-physical power connectivity is established via global net connections (globalNetConnect), and structured, layer-specific peripheral pin placements are defined—allocating data inputs (A and B) to the left on Metal3, outputs (Y) to the right on Metal3, the clock network (clk) to the top on Metal2, and control signals (Opcode) to the bottom on Metal1—before the initialized state is committed to disk.

💻 Power Planning

Loading...

Power-planning: The power planning phase begins by restoring the previously initialized design database (init_design.inn.dat) and defining the structural parameters for the Power Delivery Network (PDN). A robust power mesh configuration is programmatically synthesized using a TCL loop that targets orthogonal metal layers to minimize IR drop across the die. The script dynamically allocates vertical power and ground stripes on the even-numbered Metal4 layer (generating 16 strip sets) and horizontal stripes on the odd-numbered Metal5 layer (generating 6 strip sets). These stripes utilize a wire width and spacing of 0.1 um, maintain a symmetrical 2 um offset from the core boundaries, and extend completely to the design edge with explicitly enabled power ports for external connectivity. Following mesh synthesis, the standard cell rows are integrated into the global power grid using the special routing (sroute) command. This creates follow-pins and establishes physical connections between the standard cell core pins on Metal1 and the upper-layer power stripes, ensuring reliable vertical via-stacking spanning from Metal1 through Metal4. Finally, a Design Rule Check (DRC) violation clearance is executed, the temporary peripheral routing blockages are purged to open up the routing tracks for subsequent design phases, and the complete power-planned database is saved.

💻 Placement

Loading...

Standard Cell Placement and Pre-CTS Optimization: The placement phase is initiated by restoring the power-planned design database (power_design.inn.dat) to prepare the netlist for physical cell instantiation. To ensure optimal routability and timing closure, the placement engine is configured with stringent constraints via setPlaceMode, specifying high effort for both global congestion management and global timing optimization, while explicitly enabling clock power-driven placement to minimize downstream clock tree power dissipation. Following setup, the standard cells are physically mapped to the row sites using the core place_design command, followed by detailed wire-length optimization and localized Engineering Change Order (ECO) cell displacement limits. Immediately post-placement, a pre-Clock Tree Synthesis optimization (optDesign -preCTS) is executed to fix initial setup timing violations and design rule violations (DRVs) by resizing gates or inserting buffers before the clock tree is built. Finally, comprehensive design health diagnostics are performed—generating path, slack, and DRV reports for the top 50 critical paths, alongside localized routing congestion and cell density maps—before committing the placement-closed database to disk.

💻 CTS

Loading...

Clock Tree Synthesis and Clock Concurrent Optimization: The Clock Tree Synthesis (CTS) phase is initiated by restoring the placement-closed design database (place_design.inn.dat) to establish the physical layout of the 4-bit ALU's synchronous elements. To achieve a highly optimized and balanced clock distribution network, the synthesis engine is configured to use Clock Concurrent Optimization (ccopt) mode via setCTSMode. Stringent design targets are applied to the clock tree properties, explicitly bounding the global target clock skew to 0.05 ns and restricting the maximum clock transition time to 0.20 ns. A dedicated clock tree specification file (ccopt.spec) is then dynamically generated and sourced to define the clock roots, sinks, and skew groups. Executing the core ccopt_design -cts command synthesizes the physical clock tree by inserting balancing buffers and inverters, which is immediately followed by post-CTS timing optimization (optDesign -postCTS) to resolve hold time violations and clean up setup slacks. Finally, comprehensive post-CTS static timing analysis is executed to generate setup, hold, and design rule violation (DRV) reports for the top 50 critical paths, alongside specialized clock tree structure and skew group summaries, before saving the design database.

💻 Routing

Loading...

Detail Routing and Post-Route Optimization: The physical design implementation transitions to the routing phase by restoring the clock-tree-synthesized database (ccopt_design.inn.dat). To achieve robust signal integrity and timing closure, the NanoRoute engine is configured to run concurrently as timing-driven and Signal Integrity (SI)-driven, while the multi-cut via insertion effort is set to high via setNanoRouteMode to enhance manufacturing yield and reduce electromigration risks. The core routeDesign command is executed to complete global and detailed routing across the design's signal nets. Following wire instantiation, the analysis mode is explicitly shifted to On-Chip Variation (onChipVariation) to accurately model microscopic structural differences across the silicon die. Post-route optimization (optDesign -postRoute) is then performed sequentially for both setup and hold criteria to clean up residual timing violations and crosstalk effects introduced by actual wire parasitics. Finally, sign-off verification is conducted—running extensive post-route setup and hold timing analyses for the top 50 paths, alongside geometric Design Rule Checks (verify_drc) and physical connectivity checks (verifyConnectivity)—before committing the routed database to disk.

💻 FILL

Loading...

Chip Finishing, Metal Filling, and GDSII Stream Out: The final phase of the physical design flow focuses on layout completion and manufacturing preparation, beginning with the recovery of the fully routed database (route_design.inn.dat). To establish structural continuity across the standard cell rows, physical-only filler cells and decoupling capacitors—ranging from FILL1 to FILL64 and DECAP10—are systematically populated into empty row spaces using the addFiller command, resolving well-continuity and baseline density requirements before checking layout integrity via checkPlace. Following cell layout closure, planar density rules mandated by the foundry are addressed through automated dummy metal fill generation (addMetalFill), with the maximum length of the fill structures constrained to 10 um to mitigate parasitic capacitance overhead on active routing tracks. Physical sign-off verification is then conducted using verifyGeometry to catch residual Design Rule Check (DRC) violations, and layout artifacts are cleared. Finally, the tape-out ready database is converted into a physical hardware format via streamOut, exporting a compressed GDSII file (alu_4bit.gds.gz) by mapping standard unit metrics, unique cell designations, and merging the active design hierarchy directly with the foundational PDK standard cell layout database (gsclib045.gds).
